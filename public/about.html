The story

Nothing I write on paper will live beyond me.

My legal career has been both demanding and rewarding. I have spent years in courtrooms and at desks, crafting arguments, building cases, serving clients. It is honorable work. But I have come to understand something that took time to see clearly: nothing I do in that work will live beyond me in the memories of those I love.

That is not a counsel of despair. It is a call to build something else alongside it. Something that matters in a different register. Something that serves the Body of Christ and shows my children — all ten of them — that they have an integral part to play in the growth and maturation of Orthodoxy in the American land.

That is why I built AgaPay.

"I want to create something worthwhile — something that makes a tangible difference in people's lives."

The need

The Church deserved better than what existed.

When I looked at the giving platforms available to Orthodox parishes — Tithe.ly, Pushpay, GoFundMe — I saw tools built for a different Christianity entirely. They did not know what a panakhida was. They had never heard of the Typikon. They were not built for the liturgical rhythm of our life, for the feast days and soul Saturdays and Great Lenten appeals that shape how Orthodox Christians give.

I am a tonsured Reader in ROCOR. I have stood in the nave and held a taper at Pascha. I have submitted names for commemoration at the proskomedia. I know this life from the inside — and that is not something any competitor can replicate by hiring a consultant or adding a feature.

AgaPay was built from within the Tradition. Every feature, every word, every design decision was made by someone who lives it. That is its foundation and its competitive advantage.

The vision

A platform that outlasts its founder.

Holy Orthodoxy is young in the American land. The parishes being built today, the monasteries being established, the children being raised in the faith — these are the foundations of something that will endure for centuries if we tend it faithfully.

I want AgaPay to be part of that tending. A piece of infrastructure that makes it easier for parishes to be financially sustainable, for monasteries to receive the support they need, for the faithful to give with the same intentionality and reverence they bring to every other aspect of liturgical life.

And I want my children to see that their father built something in service of the Church — something real, something useful, something that carries the faith forward. That is worth more to me than any verdict or contract.
